Research

Dr.-Ing. candidate · TU Dresden

From experiment to service

Operational questions applied to quantum links.

The physics of quantum communication is maturing faster than its operations. Entanglement can be distributed and keys can be exchanged — under controlled conditions, by the people who built the apparatus. My doctoral research concerns what a network operator would ask next: how a quantum resource is provisioned, how its quality is measured and its degradation detected, how it is allocated when demand exceeds supply, and which guarantees can defensibly be attached to it.

I approach these questions from the systems side, through the engineering built for QD-CamNetz: resource orchestration, verification workflows, and the timing infrastructure on which deterministic services depend. The premise throughout is that the quantum layer is neither exotic nor exempt — it is a component with an interface, a failure model and a cost, and it must be engineered as one.

Themes · Three threads

Lines of inquiry

Entanglement as an allocatable resource

Entanglement is consumed by use, degrades in storage and cannot be copied — properties no classical resource-management model accommodates. This thread examines how quantum-classical networks should utilize and share entanglement, and how its scarcity reshapes the strategic behaviour of the parties that depend on it.

Security and randomness as network services

A network that produces quantum randomness and quantum-secured keys has acquired new service classes, and with them new engineering questions. These include efficient error-rate estimation in quantum key distribution, quantum entropy pools with provable properties for cryptographic applications, and quantum-resistant architectures for constrained IoT devices that will never terminate a quantum link of their own.

Time as the binding constraint

Every guarantee in the preceding threads is inherited from synchronization. This thread examines the design tradeoffs of quantum time synchronization for future industrial networks and the sub-nanosecond mechanisms that allow a distributed testbed to behave as a single deterministic system.

Software · Applied output

open5gs-qrandom-mod

An applied output of the randomness research.

An adaptation of Open5GS — a widely deployed open-source 5G core — that sources its entropy from quantum random number generation. It is the concrete form of a question the research keeps returning to: if the network produces quantum randomness as a resource, where, in standard core-network software, is it consumed?
